Types of Virtual Organs

Virtual organs span across multiple systems in the body, each addressing different challenges in healthcare and research. The five most widely developed examples are the heart, liver, kidney, lungs, and brain. Together, they form the foundation for future whole-body digital twins.

Heart

The virtual heart is one of the earliest and most advanced organ models. It simulates both the electrical activity that drives heart rhythms and the mechanical forces that pump blood. These models are used to study arrhythmias, to test pacemakers and defibrillators, and to plan catheter ablation surgeries. By creating patient-specific heart models, doctors can predict how an individual will respond to treatment before entering the operating room. Virtual hearts are also central to precision cardiology, where digital twins are being developed to improve diagnosis and treatment planning

Liver

The virtual liver has become a cornerstone for pharmacology and toxicology. Subramanian (2020) describes a liver digital twin that integrates metabolic pathways, bile acid circulation, and mitochondrial activity. This model successfully predicted how genetic disorders or drug-induced liver injuries unfold, and was even validated against clinical data. Such approaches provide a framework for developing drugs more safely and efficiently.

Kidney

The kidney is essential for filtering waste and regulating electrolytes, and its dysfunction is central to diseases like chronic kidney disease. Virtual kidneys have been developed to optimize dialysis treatments and to simulate the impact of drugs on renal clearance. Large-scale initiatives such as the Virtual Kidney Project combine distributed computing and detailed modeling to capture kidney physiology at cellular, tissue, and organ levels. These models hold promise for improving personalized care in nephrology.

Lung

The lungs, with their intricate branching airways and gas-exchange systems, are another focus of virtual organ modeling. Virtual lungs are used to study conditions such as asthma, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS). They are also valuable in optimizing mechanical ventilation strategies, allowing clinicians to simulate how different airflow or pressure settings affect patient outcomes. These models became especially relevant during the COVID-19 pandemic, where virtual testing helped refine respiratory support systems.

Brain

The virtual brain offers tools to model neural networks and conditions such as epilepsy. Projects like the Virtual Epileptic Patient use brain scans and network modeling to simulate seizure propagation, helping surgeons decide where to operate. Similar methods are being applied to neurodegenerative diseases and brain stimulation therapies.
